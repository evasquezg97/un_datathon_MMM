Medellín’s journey toward sustainable tourism

Understanding the impact of tourism and developing tools to democratize its benefits by focusing on sustainable initiative.

MMM team, de Medallo pal mundo

10 de noviembre de 2024

Does the current information about tourism businesses and initiatives accurately reflect their rapid growth and the efforts they are making toward sustainability and their impact on the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs)?

NO. Medellín is currently experiencing a surge in tourism, but the sources of information available to visitors and investors tend to favour larger, well-established businesses and popular initiatives. This bias creates a barrier to discovering smaller, sustainable businesses and grassroots projects that could offer unique, environmentally responsible experiences. As a result, the full diversity of Medellín’s tourism offerings remains hidden, limiting opportunities for a more balanced and inclusive tourism growth that aligns with sustainable practices.

There are three key problems

1. The current search methods are bias to larger initiatives.

2. Business and initiative information is outdated and scarce.

3. Current tourism information provides little to no insight into its impact on quality of life, green spaces, and environmentally friendly areas, nor on how these aspects affect the city's economy and society.

Solutions

1. Create a platform to leverage geospatial information to recommend city locations based on user queries.

2.Develop tourism impact index.

The story of Medellín is one of resilience and transformation, flowing alongside the banks of the Medellín River

By the mid-20th century, Medellín was the thriving industrial heart of Colombia. The city’s growth continued, drawing migrants from rural areas in search of opportunities. The Medellín River was lined with bustling factories and workshops, driving economic prosperity. Yet, as the city expanded, so did the complexity of its social landscape

The 1980s brought a dark chapter. Medellín became synonymous with drug cartels, violence, and fear, largely due to the influence of Pablo Escobar’s cartel. Crime rates skyrocketed, and life in the city became precarious. Despite these challenges, the people of Medellín held onto their spirit of “berraquera”—a local term that embodies resilience and determination

Entering the 2000s, Medellín underwent a remarkable transformation. The spirit of “berraquera” drove a new vision for the city, one focused on social progress and urban renewal. Investment poured into public infrastructure, with projects like the Medellín Metro, the innovative Metrocable, and a network of public libraries and parks. Medellín’s leaders worked to bridge social divides, ensuring that the city’s most vulnerable communities felt included in its progress

Today, Medellín has evolved into a vibrant centre for technology, entrepreneurship, and tourism. It’s known as an innovation hub, where startups thrive, and technology companies find a welcoming ecosystem. The once-industrial riverbanks have been revitalized, with green spaces, cultural venues, and modern infrastructure that draw locals and visitors alike. Medellín’s story—from an industrial town to a city marred by violence to a globally recognized hub for innovation—is a testament to the resilience, resourcefulness, and unbreakable spirit of its people
